A TRUSTEE’S VIEW OF WASHINGTON AND LEE

 

 

When it was suggested that I write
the introduction to this year’s
University Report from the point of
view of the Trustees, I jumped at the
chance.

For the Trustees find these to be
exciting and eventful times at
Washington and Lee. I can speak
with particular conviction since I
have recently been able to discuss
this very subject individually with
most of the Trustees.

Washington and Lee is blessed
with a highly dedicated, concerned,
and hard-working Board, and
without exception these men—all of
whom are most perceptive—are
pleased with the state of the
University.

As they see it, our educational
standards continue to have that
margin of excellence we constantly
seek. The curriculum—always a
strong one—continues to be
broadened through the addition of
new courses and, during the past
year, a new major. Of particular
interest is a successful program in
pre-professional ethics that cuts
across the College, the Commerce
School, and the Law School. There
are new programs to train faculty
and students in computer science,
and a new, much more powerful
computer is being installed.

Washington and Lee has begun a
major in East Asian Studies with
numerous courses in East Asian
languages, religion, art and history
available for majors and non-majors
alike.

The Trustees are particularly
pleased that emphasis is placed
throughout the College on
improving writing and

communication skills. In our own
businesses and professions, we have
all learned that these skills are
among the more important
contributions to a successful career,
and we have all had experience with
bright young men who are singularly
lacking in those abilities.

The faculty is loyal and strong
with the emphasis remaining on
teaching. There has been no
compromise on the faculty-student
ratio which remains about | to 11.
Faculty salaries have been improved
and are at a good level.

Our student body continues to be
a good one, and our applications for
admission evidence a better trend in
number and in quality than that of
many other private colleges. We
have chosen to remain all male at the
undergraduate level, a choice which
has had no discernible effect on
admission applications.

‘The Honor System is strong. Each
new generation of students re-
examines it and takes its own steps to
keep it that way.

In Bob Huntley we are convinced
that we truly have a President for all
seasons—one who relates in
inimitable fashion and with equal
skill with such diverse constituents as
students, faculty, parents, trustees,
alumni, donors, and friends of the
University. We are continually
impressed with his enthusiasm, his
knowledge of all facets of University
operations, his administrative skills,
and his articulateness. He is the right
man at the right time for
Washington and Lee.

 

Another source of pride is, of
course, our accomplishments during
Phase I of our Development
Program for the ’70s. This
program—unusually ambitious when
it was formulated—called for raising
$36 million in new funds by the end
of 1976. In fact, we raised almost
$37.5 million, over seven percent of
which was contributed by the Board
of Trustees with all members
participating. Unfortunately, the
ravages of inflation prevented this
amount from doing all we had
planned, but we were able to build

 

 

 

 

 

or at least begin construction of all
of the physical facilities announced
as objectives for that first portion of
the program.

In place are the new Jonathan
Westervelt Warner Athletic Center;
the new law building, Lewis Hall; a
three-building, on-campus
apartment complex for
upperclassmen and law students;
and major campus utility, service,
and landscaping improvements.
Begun was the new $9-million
undergraduate library and the
renovation of the Lee-Jackson
House. The building which has been
the home of our biology, physics,
and engineering departments was
named Parmly Hall in honor and

recognition of a gift from Mrs Ruth «

Parmly of New York City.

The Trustees are particularly
grateful to loyal alumni and parents,
led by 498 Lee Associates, who have
increased the total of the Annual
Fund by more than 100 percent in
the years since 1970 when Phase I
began. Without this increasing
support we could not have
maintained the balanced budgets we
have been able to achieve.

But despite all the achievements
of Phase I, the Trustees recognize
that the job is by no means finished.

Funds for the completion of the
new library must be found. The old
McCormick Library building must
be renovated to serve as a new home
for the School of Commerce,
Economics, and Politics. Newcomb
Hall will need renovation after the
Commerce School moves out. And
there are miscellaneous other utility
and campus improvements that will
need to be made.

Even more urgent is the need for
additional endowment. Phase I did
not raise as much for endowment as
we had planned. At the same time a
declining securities market has
severely eroded our existing
endowment, and annual operating
costs are escalating due to inflation.
Each new building placed in service
also brings with it a further increase
in annual costs.

The only way we can hope to
offset these multiplying costs is by a
significant enlargement of our
endowment.

As Bob Huntley has pointed out
in a recent letter to alumni, parents,

_.and friends, Washington and Lee is
/not on easy street. Even the generous
‘bequest from John Lee Pratt, instead

of adding to what many erroneously
presume to be already bountiful
resources, actually has only partially
filled an already existing gap.

The figures have been cited
before, but they need to be repeated
again. The 1975-76 survey report
(the most recent such study) of the
Council for Financial Aid to
Education lists Washington and
Lee’s endowment as $12,373 per
student. Comparative endowment
figures in the same CFAE report for
a number of other universities are:

So, our job clearly is not done. We
are determined to secure an
adequate endowment for
Washington and Lee—one that will
permit us to maintain our position as
an outstanding small private
University. If this is not done, our
margin of excellence will soon begin
to erode.

To this end, the Trustees are
planning now for Phase II of our
Development Program for the ’70s.
When announced, it will
undoubtedly call for raising more
new capital than had originally been
thought necessary when the overall
program was formulated—and a
major part of this will be for
increased endowment.

We are confident it can be done.

BOARD OF TRUSTEES

The Board of Trustees has 22 members. Each trustee is elected to a
maximum of two consecutive six-year terms. The trustees elect all
members, with the alumni body nominating one candidate every two
years. The President of the University is a member during his tenure.

THE ROBERT E. LEE ASSOCIATES

The Robert E. Lee Associates was established by the
Board of Trustees in 1967 to identify and recognize
leadership in support of the University’s educational
program. Alumni and friends are cordially invited to join
this fellowship.

Qualification for Membership: An alumnus, parent, or
friend may join the Robert E. Lee Associates by
contributing $1,000 or more to the University through its
Annual Fund for support of current Operations in the
educational and general budget. This gift may be paid in

June 30.

General’s Council: A Lee Associate becomes a member
of the General’s Council for cumulative gifts totaling
$10,000 or more in 12 consecutive years or less, or for a
pledge to accomplish this result. Thus, a donor may
fulfill this requirement by giving—or by pledging to give
—$1,000 in any 10 years out of 12, by making larger gifts
at less frequent intervals, or by a single gift of $10,000 or
more. Listed here are members of the General’s Council
and those Lee Associates who made a gift to the Annua

THE ESTATE PLANNING PROGRAM

Washington and Lee’s deferred
giving program provides the means
by which donors may share some of
their capital with Washington and
Lee, either by will or by another
form of gift to take effect in the
future.

The importance of the program
to the University can be measured in
part by numbers. During the 1976-
77 fiscal year, Washington and Lee
received in distributions from estates
of alumni and friends (other than
from the estate of John Lee Pratt)
$4,525,829.08, the largest amount of
such gifts in many years. The donors
of these funds were

Ada S. Boyce

Mrs. Lucille W. Chaffin
Christopher Chenery ’09
John Sims Edmondson ’20
Howard Gibbons ’21L
Richard E. Gooch ’30

W. H. Howe

Eben Jackson °33

Miriam Capterton A. McClure
H. Graham Morison ’30

Figures alone, however, do not
indicate the full importance to
Washington and Lee of the
increased participation by its friends
in this program. The willingness of
alumni and other supporters to
make this University a part of their
estate plans is a gauge of the success
of Washington and Lee both in
preserving the best of the past, the
greatest of our traditions and ideals,
and in providing educational
leadership for the future, training
today’s young to meet tomorrow’s
challenge. No university can remain
strong if it does not perform both of
these functions.

A deferred gift, a gift of part of
one’s estate, is a memorial to the
donor, and honors what Washington
and Lee has been. It also, however,
represents a commitment to what

44

Washington and Lee will be. These
gifts are a concrete expression of the
donors’ faith that Washington and
Lee will continue to provide the best
in education in the liberal arts and
law. Naturally these expressions of
faith assist greatly in enabling the
University to meet the expectations
of the donors.

The purposes for which deferred
gifts are made are as varied as the
interests of the givers. Many gifts of
capital are unrestricted--used as
additions to endowments or for
capital improvements at the
discretion of the Trustees. Others,
depending on their size, fund
scholarships or professorships, build
or restore buildings, provide funds
for lectures or the acquisition of
books, scientific materials, or works
of art. The history of the University
and its physical site abound with the
names of donors.

Deferred gifts range in size from
$500 or $1,000 to many millions of
dollars. Each is an equally valued
expression of the donor’s pride and
faith in Washington and Lee.

Almost as varied as the purposes
and sizes of deferred gifts are the
ways in which they are made. Most
commonly they take the form of a

provision in the donor’s will.
Younger alumni, especially,
frequently use life insurance as the
vehicle for their estate planned gifts.
Almost $100,000 of policies payable
to Washington and Lee are known to
the University to be in force now;
slightly more than $100,000 has
been received from insurance
policies in recent years.

More often, donors are creating
living trusts which provide a life
income to them or to their
beneficiary with the principal
ultimately coming to Washington
and Lee. More than $1,000,000 is
now in our Pooled Income Fund,
which, quarterly, returns income to
those who have merged their gifts to
the University in this professionally
managed trust. Approximately
$1,000,000 is now held in
separately invested annuity trusts or
unitrusts, each designed to meet the
particular needs of the individual
donor and each providing funds on
which Washington and Lee can base
its plans for the future.

Professional management of
funds, avoidance of capital gains tax
on sales of securities and other
income and estate tax advantages
make these trusts particularly
desirable in a time of economic and
market uncertainty. Information
about any of these deferred giving
plans is available from the Office of
University Development.

Washington and Lee has been
built on the love and pride of those
who know it and on the tangible
expressions of this love and pride
which many have made through the
years. [The University is grateful to
all those who, now, through their
estate plans are building the
institution that in the twenty-first
century will continue to be an
institution worthy of the names it
