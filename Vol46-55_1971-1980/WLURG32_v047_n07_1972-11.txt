A QUALITY OF LEADERSHIP

RosertT E. R. HUNTLEY
President
Washington and Lee University

 

 

This may be the most significant Report of Gifts that
Washington and Lee University has ever published. In
terms of achievement, the past year was one of notable
superlatives. It was the year during which Washington
and Lee received the largest gift total in its history. And
it was the year during which our Trustees announced a
comprehensive projection of the financial requirements
the University must meet in the decade ahead and the
role that giving must play in meeting them.

In the pages that follow, Mr. John M. Stemmons of
the University’s Board of Trustees describes and com-
ments on giving in 1971-72. Mr. Stemmons is chairman
of the Achievement Council, the group charged with di-
rection of all the University’s various fund-raising activi-
ties. Mr. Stemmon’s enthusiastic leadership is a major
factor in the large stride already made toward our initial
fund-raising objective, $36,000,000 by 1976.

Washington and Lee’s goals for this decade are am-
bitious, but they represent no more than the minimum
required to carry this University—healthy and vigorous—
into a future of a quality comparable with that of the

past. That task can be accomplished only through pri-

vate giving. I believe, as our Trustees believe, that we
owe it to this University’s family and to all who love
her to state candidly her needs and to state with equal
candor what your University expects of you.

The hope, the expectation, is that each supporter of
the University will address himself first to the question
of how much he can reasonably expect to give, on an
annual basis, for operating expenses—usually through
the Alumni Fund or the Parents’ Fund. ‘Then our hope
would be that he will address himself to the possibility
of making an additional commitment to the University
to assist in meeting capital needs—the physical and en-
dowment requirements of the decade.

Virtually from its beginning, Washington and Lee's
history has borne eloquent witness to the generosity of
those who shared precisely such a common concern for
the future of their institution. All manner of giving has
shaped our strength, including those testamentary acts
of faith which unite generations in the enduring fulfill-
ment that characterizes Washington and Lee.

Every friend who gives to Washington and Lee, who
invests in its future, should expect the University to
make the best possible use of his gift. Indeed he has a
right to expect that we will make the fullest possible
use of every resource we have—whether those resources
come to us as gifts, as income from our endowment, or
as our students’ tuition. We are committed to those ob-

: jectives of wisdom and prudence in ways that I believe

all friends of Washington and Lee will find reassuring.

One of the most inspiring developments of the past
two years has been the growing nucleus of voluntary
leadership joining our Trustees in seeking to achieve
these unprecedented financial goals for the ’70s. The list
of these alumni and other leaders is impressive in num-
bers alone. But more than that, they represent a quality
of leadership that only the very strongest institutions
could ever hope to muster in their behalf.

I extend the most profound personal gratitude to all
who have helped make this record of progress during
the past year. And I invite them, and all others who care
about Washington and Lee and its future, to move for-
ward with us against new challenges with vigor and

confidence.

ROBERT E. R. HUNTLEY
President
Washington and Lee University

UNPRECEDENTED GENEROSITY

JOHN M. STEMMons, ’31
Chairman

Washington and Lee
Achievement Council

 

 

Any record year is looked upon with pride. The un-
precedented generosity with which the Washington and
Lee family responded to the needs of the University last
year makes 1971-72 our best effort so far.

Our achievement was notable in two respects: the
quality of leadership and the impressive monetary re-
sult.

Capable and dedicated leadership was seen in James
H. Bierer, ’40, chairman of the Alumni Fund. He and
his corps of class agents led the effort to an all-time high,
exceeding their goal and posting a 23% increase over
last year. No other single factor had as great an effect
upon the financial health of Washington and Lee as
did the Alumni Fund which accounted for 43% of the
gift revenues needed to balance the 1971-72 educational
and general budget.

The Parents’ Fund, under the leadership of Dr. Al-
bert Preston and his five class agents, also exceeded its

 

goal and provided once again evidence of the interest
and faith of parents whose sons study at Washington
and Lee.

The Achievement Council, composed of 28 men, made
significant progress in its first official year of activity.
Through this Council, Washington and Lee seeks to
realize its most pressing capital requirements. Martin P.
Burks, °32, and the 42 members of the Estate Planning
Council were successful in completing all necessary ar-
rangements for beginning an aggressive deferred gifts
and bequests program.

Most important, however, is to note that all these
men are volunteers who have stepped forward to accept
hard jobs for the University—jobs which they discharged
last year with skill and effectiveness.

On the opposite page are two reports.

The first indicates the actual cash that Washington
and Lee received in gifts and pledge payments during
the 1971-72 fiscal year. Included in this report are
$372,133 received for the Alumni Fund, $363,751 of
which is for current support. Of the $68,152 received
through the Parents’ Fund, $66,846 is for current sup-
port. ‘I'he second report reflects the progress that we
are making toward meeting the 1976 objective of $3,-
500,000 in current support plus $32,500,000 in capital
support for a total of $36,000,000. Building on the base
of $11.5 million in combined gifts from all members of
the Board of ‘Trustees, the Washington and Lee Develop-
ment Program has received an additignal $7.7 million in
commitments, as of June 30, 1972, bringing it to 58%
of its 1976 objective.

This Gift Report publication clearly expresses the
high regard and esteem in which the University is held
by its family and other constituencies. I think that
Washington and Lee fully merits this wonderful sup-
port, based both on its past and upon what it can achieve
in the future.

—__arrie.

Joun M. STEMMoNs, ’31
Chairman
Washington and Lee Achievement Council

special place in the heritage of Washington and Lee.

Washington and Lee exists for one reason: To provide
the best possible education to the young men who
come to W&L. It is through its graduates’ contribution
to society that Washington and Lee serves mankind.

THE COLONNADE CLUB

One of the most significant factors in the suc-
cess of both the Alumni Fund and the Parents’
Fund in 1971-72 was the establishment of the
Colonnade Club to recognize donors of $100-$999
in support of current, operations. In 1971-72,
more than 1,200 alumni, parents, and friends
became members with gifts that totaled over

$185,000.

A measure of the Colonnade Club’s impact on
the Alumni Fund is the fact that gifts at the
Colonnade Club level accounted for 46.4% of
the total dollars and 29.9% of the donors. Colon-
nade Club gifts to the Parents’ Fund accounted
for 35.6% of the dollars and 43.8% of the donors.

In the years ahead, the Colonnade Club will
play a major role in keeping Washington and
Lee excellent educationally as well as sound

financially.

THE RICHMOND TROPHY

The Richmond Trophy was presented to the
Washington and Lee Alumni Association by the
Richmond chapter at the annual meeting of the
Association on May 13, 1972. It will be awarded
annually to the academic class, graduated within
the last 50 years, which achieves proportionately
the highest participation in the Alumni Fund.

The class of 1942A was the first winner of the
trophy, with a participation rate of 43.1% in
the 1971-72 Alumni Fund. Frank L. LaMotte,
Class Agent, will receive the trophy for the class
at Fall Reunions, October 28.

The year of the award, the Class Agent’s name,
the class numerals and the winning percentage
will be engraved on the trophy each year.

Because of the great difference in size between
most law and academic classes, which makes
comparison of performance difficult, only under-
graduate classes are eligible to compete for the
Richmond Trophy. It is hoped that an alumnus,
an alumni chapter, a family or an entire class
will present a trophy to encourage similar com-
petition among Law Class Agents.

MEMORIAL GIFTS

Many alumni and friends of Wash-
ington and Lee contribute to the
University’s capital and _ current
operating needs in the form of me-
morials to fellow alumni, relatives
and friends.

In 1972 Benjamin W. Partlow ’21
established a scholarship fund in me-
mory of his wife, Jane McCauley
Partlow, with the express purpose
of encouraging other alumni to make

 

similar gifts in memory of their wives.
Mr. Partlow’s concept of the fund
is that each gift shall remain a separ-
ate endowment, but that the income
from all shall be pooled to create
scholarship awards for one or more
students with unusual academic or
personal talents.
