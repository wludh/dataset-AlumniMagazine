THOUGHTS ABOUT WASHINGTON AND LEE

In last year’s University Report, I presented highlights
of the past year derived from reports compiled by the
deans and other administrative officers. This year I would
like to take a little different approach. I want to share with
you some reflections of my own about Washington and Lee
and its institutional objectives. Most of you are familiar with
Washington and Lee’s essential qualities. But I think you
will agree with me that it can be rewarding from time to
time to recapitulate those characteristics that make Wash-
ington and Lee worthy of our support and affection.

Washington and Lee’s commitment to the liberal arts
tradition in education is strong and unwavering, the more
so in these days when the tradition is under considerable
attack, tacitly or explicitly, from various sources.

I am aware that the term “liberal arts” is neither distinc- : e — |
tive nor self-defining. While I am sure that no definition I Ss i ad
could offer is likely to be adequate or complete, I believe PRESIDENT Rosert E. R. HUNTLEY
that at Washington and Lee we mean by the term “liberal
arts tradition” something like the following:

 

A commitment to the premise that education of the
youthful mind is best achieved by a curricular program
during the undergraduate years which requires rigorous
inquiry and study of one of the disciplines which time has
proven to be intellectually demanding, and which simul-
taneously requires of the student at least some significant
study in a number of other similar disciplines. The objec-

tive—never quite accomplished, of course—is the achieve-
ment of intellectual discipline and self-confidence through
deep involvement and approximate mastery of at least one
demanding area of study, and at the same time the achieve-
ment of some perspective on what might be called the
human condition, particularly the accumulated achieve-
ments of the human intellect, the power of reason, its
strengths and its limitations. The liberal arts tradition to
which we adhere would hold that this kind of educational
objective is not a luxury, as some would assert, but rather
an extremely efficient way to prepare for a useful and ful-

filling life.

In my judgment, the major weakness of the liberal arts

tradition over the years has been the occasional but persis-
tent notion, both in and out of academia, that it can only
be successfully pursued if abstracted from reality, that it is
somehow sullied if it is conjoined with studies which per-
tain to specific career objectives of the student. At Wash-
ington and Lee, we have for more than a century rejected
the notion that any such dichotomy exists. On the contrary,
we have embraced the opposite notion: that professional or
pre-professional education is best achieved if begun at the
undergraduate level in a way which closely interweaves
studies usually thought of as oriented to the professions
with studies traditionally regarded as liberal arts. Such in-
terweaving occurs through curricular organization and
teaching methods. Here, we think it occurs best in a small
college atmosphere where close interdisciplinary relation-

ships can occur among faculty and where the relationship

between faculty and student can go far beyond the limits
of the classroom.

As you would expect of an institution with the kinds of
objectives I have alluded to, the vast majority of our stu-
dents both now and in the past have found their way into
the professions. We take great pride in their achievements.
Far out of proportion to their number, they consistently
achieve distinction of the best kind in business, in law, in
medicine, in the ministry, in teaching and research, in jour-
nalism, and not infrequently in the arts.

_ We try very hard to tie everything we do here to our
educational objectives. For example, we try to attract and re-
tain a faculty of persons whose principal objective in life is
teaching. We have a number of programs which encourage
and assist our faculty in research and writing, and their
achievements 1n these pursuits are often significant. But we
never think of these pursuits as ends in themselves, though
it might be quite appropriate for some other kinds of in-
stitutions to do so. Rather we approach them as effective
ways to strengthen our faculty as teachers, never believing
for one moment that professional achievement and profes-
sional pride by a faculty member need be inconsistent with
commitment to the institutional objectives of effective
teaching.

Similarly, our athletic program is justified by its rela-
tionship to our educational objectives. More than twenty
years ago, at a time when such a move was rather unusual,
Washington and Lee moved boldly away from the kind of
specially financed athletic program which sometimes tends
to become almost a separate enterprise revolving in an
orbit of its own. We probably spend no less money on ath-
letics—perhaps more—than we would be spending under
the so-called “subsidized approach,” but we spend the
money in an effort to assure each student the opportunity
to participate and compete to the fullest level of his ability
and desire.

One further point I cannot close without mentioning.
Since the time when General Lee was president here fol-
lowing the Civil War, the responsibility entrusted to the stu-
dents for self-governance has been extraordinarily broad.
A wide array of student activities falls entirely under their
aegis, and this fact is not a result of some token bow in the di-
rection of student activists of the ’60’s, but pre-dates that
turbulent era by many decades. The most significant aspect
of student life here is the pervasive influence of the system
of honor, which, again, dates from the Lee years. I do not
believe that there are many places in which the concept of
honor is taken so seriously or guarded so jealously by the
students as is the case at Washington and Lee. Again, this
notion of an honorable community is closely related to our
educational objectives, for we think that the teaching-
learning process flourishes best in a situation where
mutual trust and respect form the bedrock of relationships
within the community.

As for the future, I am convinced that all higher educa-
tion institutions in America, particularly independent
ones, will be well advised to concentrate effort where past
achievements demonstrate special capacity and strength
rather than to proliferate their programs in a futile effort
to achieve every worthy educational objective in a single in-
stitution.

Some who want to make a por-
tion of their capital available to
Washington and Lee, either for the
University’s unrestricted use or to
further a particular interest are doing
so, not through their wills, but by
making life income gifts. Such gifts
may take different forms (an example
of a typical gift to Washington and
Lee’s Pooled Income Fund is in the
accompanying box), but they have
certain common characteristics.

Each donor of a life income gift
parts with some capital but retains or
gives to another the income produced
by this capital. Each life income gift
enables Washington and Lee to an-
ticipate in its planning an identifi-
able future addition to its funds.
Life income gifts enable a donor to
remove assets from his estate, saving
some of the costs and delays incident
to estate administration, and to ob-
tain professional management of his
assets during his lifetime. Finally,
there are significant tax incentives to

that encourage a donor to be more
