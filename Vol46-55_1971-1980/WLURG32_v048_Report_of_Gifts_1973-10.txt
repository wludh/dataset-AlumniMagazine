PROGRESS AND PROMISE

 

With gifts and pledges totalling $21,707,320 at the

close of the University’s 1972-73 fiscal year, the Washing-
ton and Lee Development Program reached 60.3% of its
$36 million Phase I financial objective. ‘This represented
a gain of $2.5 million over the previous year’s total.

At this writing, less than 36 months remain to raise
the approximately $14.3 million in additional commit-
ments needed to reach our goal prior to the Nation’s
200th birthday in the summer of 1976. Good progress
has been made, but during the past months the rate of
that progress has slowed considerably, especially in our
programs for support of building and endowment objec-
tives. A redoubling of effort on the part of the Achieve-
ment Council team is required to regain the momentum
necessary to carry this vital undertaking to a triumphant
conclusion.

Alumni and Parents’ Fund contributions, together
with gifts from Friends, Foundations, and Business, re-
sulted in a total of $720,950 in support of the University’s
current operations, as compared with $853,833 the prev-
ious year. Gifts and pledge payments received during
1972-73 in support of endowment and physical plant de-
velopment totalled $1,352,723. ‘These receipts are shown
in the first table on the next page. ‘he second table re-

In one important area of the program, there has been
no slackening of momentum whatever. The pace of the
Alumni Fund, under the leadership of James H. Bierer,
‘40, has continued to accelerate. With an increase of
more than 10% over last year’s total, the Fund attained
an all-time record of $411,074 in contributions, exceed-
ing its goal by better than $11,000. The Parents’ Fund,
under the leadership of Robert A. Hemm, again pro-
vided strong endorsement of Washington and Lee’s pro-
grams, with gifts totalling $62,625, even though this was
somewhat short of the announced goal.

At this midpoint in the progress of our development
effort, a sound foundation has been laid for achieving the
vitally necessary goals upon which the future of Wash-
ington and Lee depends. I am confident that I speak for
all who know and love this University when I affirm that
the promise to which we are committed will be fulfilled;
it is a pledge which we have made not merely to our-
selves, but to generations to come.

THE ALUMNI FUND

JAMEs H. Bierer, ’40
Chairman
Alumni Fund

 

 

The 1972-73 Alumni Fund established all-time highs
in three categories—total contributions, number of
donors, and rate of participaticn. Against a goal of
$400,000, gifts of $411,074 set a new record for the third
consecutive year and surpassed the Fund’s goal for the
second time in succession. The 4,531 contributors ex-
ceeded the record set in 1969-70 by 245 givers, and the
participation rate of 36.5% topped the previous high of
36.3% achieved in 1966-67.

One important reason—perhaps the most important—
for the success of the 1972-73 Fund is the fact that 1,417
alumni, almost one-third of those who gave, contributed
$100 or more. This is an increase of 135 over the previous
year, 20 of whom were among the 119 members of the
Robert E. Lee Associates who gave $1,000 or more, and
115 of whom were members of the Colonnade Club.

More important than record setting, however, is the
fact that the Alumni Fund provided 7.21% of the in-
come needed to meet expenses in the University’s edu-
cational and general budget—the highest percentage in
five years.

T'wo new trophies, announced in September, have in-
troduced additional competition among academic classes
and class agents. ‘The Washington Trophy, for the lar-
gest dollar contribution, was won by the Class of 1928,
Percy Cohen, Class Agent, and the Bierer Trophy, for

participation by a class graduated within the last 10
years, was won by the Class of 1964, W. B. Ogilvie, Class
Agent. he Richmond Trophy for participation was won
by the Class of 1958, V. W. Holleman, Class Agent.

The goal for the 1973-74 Alumni Fund is $435,000, an
increase which reflects the higher cost of operations an-
ticipated in the University’s educational and general
budget.

BUSINESS AND INDUSTRY/
VIRGINIA FOUNDATION FOR
INDEPENDENT COLLEGES

The University’s friends in the
business and industrial community
contributed a significant 18.4% of the
total gift income received for sup-
port of current operations in 1972-73.
As always, the cornerstone of this sup-
port in 1972-73 was the $101,391 re-
ceived through the statewide program
of solicitation carried out by the Vir-
ginia Foundation for Independent
Colleges. Under the direction of a
board that includes 28 business lead-
ers and the presidents of the 12 in-
dependent member colleges, this or-
ganization enlisted the support of 925
Virginia business establishments to
raise a total of $1,318,600 for private
higher education. ‘Thus, in dollar
support per college and per student,
the Virginia Foundation continues to
rank first among 40 similar state and
regional organizations.

The balance of the contribution
from the business and industrial con-
stituency came in the form of direct
erants and matching gifts made
through programs specially designed
to aid the independent colleges.

During the period covered by this report Washington
and Lee received distributions from the estates of the
following alumni and friends.

Bess Rector Crist
Alice M. Dean
Wilbur C. Hall ’°15
Oscar B. Huffman
Graham Cameron Lytle ’27
S. Blount Mason ’05
Granville P. Meade
Russell B. Reynolds
William B. Russell
Esther Schlossberg
Beirne Stedman ’09

These payments, some of which were in full satisfac-
tion of the University’s share in the estates and some of
which were additions to prior distributions or partial or
beginning payments of the gift to Washington and Lee,
amounted to approximately $150,000. Reflecting as they
do lifetimes of interest in and devotion to Washington
and Lee, these gifts are received with the University’s
particular gratitude. Some are unrestricted and some are
to be used for specific purposes, furthering a particular
interest of the donor, but all perpetuate the memories of
those who loved Washington and Lee.

The University has been notified during the year of
the establishment of three trusts, the principal of which
will pass to it after the termination of certain lifetime
interests. ‘These trusts have a total present market value
of approximately $360,000.

By including Washington and Lee in their estate
plans these donors have helped assure that Washington
and Lee can maintain its traditional excellence. They are
enabling future generations to share some of their ex-
periences, values and ideals. And, because of the encour-
agement which is given by the government through the
tax laws to private support of education, their gifts repre-
sent a wise investment in America’s future.

Under the leadership of Martin P. Burks, ’32, the
Estate Planning Council of Washington and Lee, whose
names are listed below, can help alumni and friends of
the University to make plans that not only achieve their
desires to support the University but, through tax con-
servation, provide financial benefits for themselves and
their families. Information about gift plans suitable for
a particular donor’s circumstances is available from the
Office of University Development.
