THE PRICELESS ELEMENT
OF INDEPENDENCE

 

 

At a time when operating deficits have become almost
a commonplace among the nation’s independent colleges
and universities, including some of the most heavily en-
dowed, it is gratifying to report that Washington and
Lee was able to realize its balanced budget for the 1971
fiscal year.

To acknowledge that this happy result could not have
been achieved without the generous gift support of the
University’s constituency is simply to state a financial fact
of academic life. But in a period of rapid social change,
when public attitudes reveal so much uncertainty about
the role of higher education, this support bears witness
to a sincere vote of confidence by many alumni, parents,
corporations, foundations and friends. For this Washing-
ton and Lee is especially grateful.

Among those who know the University best it is im-
possible to separate its individuality, its special character
and personality, from the fact of its independence. For
almost two and a quarter centuries, Washington and Lee
has been free to exercise its own initiative, imagination
and professional judgment in shaping its educational
program—free to take the risks of experiment and in-
novation. In exchange for this right to “‘be itself,” it has
always welcomed the implicit responsibility to raise
operating funds from regular and gift sources, and has
been willing to accept the verdict of the market place in
the competition of ideas.

Through great wars and great depressions, as well as
in times of peace and prosperity, alumni and friends have
vigilantly protected this priceless element of indepen-
dence with gifts and service that have assured Washing-
ton and Lee’s financial integrity. It has happened again
in fiscal 1971, and the University extends a special word
of thanks to the many who worked so generously in its

behalf.

THE ROBERT E. LEE
ASSOCIATES

 

 

The importance of annual giving in
support of current operations receiv-
ed new emphasis in 1970-71 when ‘The
Robert E. Lee Associates, through a
committee of members and with ap-
proval of the Board of Trustees, decid-
ed to channel all contributions toward
the University’s educational and gen-
eral budget.

In making this decision the Lee As-
sociates and the Board recognized
that Washington and Lee had entered
a period of maximum need, and that
increased gift support was essential to
offset the escalating cost of operating
the University.

In another move to develop the
leadership role of ‘The Robert E. Lee
Associates, the Board created a new
category of donors to be known as
‘The General’s Council.”” Included in
this select company are those who
give, or pledge to give, the sum of
$10,000 for current operating purposes
in a span of twelve consecutive years.
On June 30, 1971, 43 Lee Associates
had become charter members of ‘The
General's Council.

During the year the number of liv-
ing members of ‘The Robert E. Lee As-
sociates rose by 33 persons to 271.
This total includes 197 alumni, 50
parents and 24 friends, 151 of whom
were contributors at the Lee Associ-
ates level ($1,000 or more in a twelve-
month period) in 1970-71.

With total contributions at an all-time high, and with
the average gift increased by 10.5% over that of 1969-70, a
review of the 1970-71 Alumni Fund provides ample cause
for satisfaction, especially in a year when so many factors
conspired to inhibit progress. It also should be reported
that the Fund fell short of its stated $325,000 goal for the
second consecutive year, and that participation dropped
by 2.4%. But even these disappointments were tempered
by the fact that two large, non-recurring gifts, made to
Washington and Lee from sources not included in the
Alumni Fund, offset the $22,000 Alumni Fund shortage
that otherwise would have contributed to a University
operating deficit.

A comparison of the 1970-71 Fund results with those
of previous years, however, gives reason for sober reflec-
tion on the purpose of the Fund and on its great po-
tential as a source of current income to the University.

Except for 1969-70, for instance, the chart in Fig. 3
shows an uninterrupted seven-year upward trend in con-

tributions, whereas the chart in Fig. 4 shows an alarming
failure to increase the degree of participation by alumni.
Herein lies a great untapped potential for future years.
At only $10 each, non-contributing alumni in 1970-71
could add almost $79,000 to the 1971-72 Fund.

Equally a cause for concern is the trend evidenced by
the chart in Fig. 5. Since 1967-68, despite the increase in
total dollars, the Fund has provided for a steadily dimin-
ishing share of the University’s educational and general
expense. Had the Fund met its $325,000 goal, this trend
would have been reversed, and the share of actual edu-
cational and general expense would have risen to 7.1%.

The $360,000 goal for 1971-72 is about half of the
amount needed to close the gap between expected in-
come from regular sources and the educational and gen-
eral expenses that are budgeted at $5,361,370 for this
year.

BUSINESS
AND INDUSTRY

Washington and Lee has one of the most dis-
tinctive and historical campuses in the country.
But it costs money to keep the campus attrac-
tive and serviceable. Preventive upkeep—as
practiced by the University—extends the
serviceability of buildings. From the operat-

ing budget comes the money to pay for the
necessary tools, machinery, and skilled personnel.

 

Relatively unfavorable economic
conditions caused a slight drop in
contributions from the business and
industrial community in 1970-71.
Nevertheless these donations, which
included $18,000 for capital purposes
and $115,000 for current expenses,
accounted for 8% of all gift income
and 19% of current support received
by the University during the year. In
addition, the Alumni Fund and the
Parents’ Fund include a combined
total of $23,064 in the form of match-
ing gifts from the organizations listed
on this page. Washington and Lee
acknowledges with gratitude the in-
terest and support of all corporations
whose gifts are so essential to its well-
being.

Contributions received through the
Virginia Foundation for Independent
Colleges form the backbone of gifts
from business and industry. Since
1952, hundreds of corporations have
responded to the efforts of the VFIC
to provide means of coordinated fi-
nancial support for twelve indepen-
dent colleges and universities. ‘These
institutions are: Bridgewater College,
Emory and Henry College, Hampden-
Sydney College, Hollins College,
Lynchburg College, Mary Baldwin
College, Randolph-Macon College,
Randolph-Macon Women’s College,
Roanoke College, Sweet Briar College,
University of Richmond, and Wash-
ington and Lee University.

Washington and Lee has benefited
ereatly from the generosity of busi-
nesses contributing to higher educa-
tion in this manner. In 1970-71, with
the Foundation receiving total sup-
port of $1,035,332 from 867 contribu-
tors, Washington and Lee’s share was
$86,500. In the 19 years of its opera-
tion, the VFIC has contributed a
total of $1,085,338 to Washington and
Lee. The VFIC’s total collections
during the same period amounted to
$12,386,000 from 10,626 contributors.
Washington and Lee is particularly
appreciative of this exceptional finan-
cial support over the years.

A list of 867 contributors is too
lengthy for this report. But the Uni-
versity acknowledges that each contri-
butor, along with the VFIC staff, has
had an important beneficial effect
upon Washington and Lee University
this year.
