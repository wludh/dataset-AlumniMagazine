Making Sure the Promise Is Kept

‘The University Report for 1978-79 began
with an analysis of the progress that
Washington and Lee was making on “keeping
its promise—the promise to remain one of the
nation’s truly superior undergraduate
teaching institutions.”

Now in 1981, only a short number of
weeks away from the targeted December 31
conclusion of the $62-million development
program, all indications point to the fact that
Washington and Lee’s alumni and friends are
making sure that the promise is kept by means
of their gifts and pledges which have brought
the program (as of the end of September) to
within $2.2 million—or 91 percent—of
completion.

Because the $2.2 million remaining is a
considerable sum, the Washington and Lee
family is being generously assisted in its quest
to keep its promise by four special incentives:

a dollar-for-dollar match for all capital
pledges (construction or endowment funds) of
$10,000 or more;

M a $250,000 challenge from the Kresge
Foundation that will be funded if $1,250,000
is committed from other sources for the
renovation of Tucker and Newcomb Halls by
December 15;

M a $90,000 offer from the Dana
Foundation, challenging the University’s
alumni to increase the Annual Fund by
attainment of certain new levels in dollars and
participation;

and an offer from the National
Endowment for the Humanities to give
Washington and Lee $200,000 on the basis of
one dollar for every three dollars committed Photographs by W. Patrick Hinely, ’73
for an endowment for the University’s
outstanding program, Society and the
Professions: Studies in Applied Ethics.

Even though the time remaining is short,

 

Washington and Lee has much going for it in
the demonstrated loyalty and generosity of its
alumni and friends. It is the intention of the
Board of Trustees to recognize that loyalty
and generosity by erecting in the new library,
sometime after December 31, a wall panel
listing the names of all donors of capital gifts
of $10,000 or more, made throughout the
course of the 10-year development program.

Washington and Lee’s development
program, of course, is simply a financial
expression of the University’s aspiration to
maintain and improve its excellence in the
development of students’ broad capacities to
learn, to understand, and to use their
education productively. The success of
Washington and Lee’s development program
will also provide a means of continuing the
University’s historic independence which in
large part is predicated on financial stability.

By achieving both objectives—
maintenance of a superior educational
program and assuring independence through
financial stability—Washington and Lee will
indeed be keeping its promise, and it is coming
about because of those whose gifts are listed
in this University Report and because of those
whose gifts in support of Washington and
Lee’s future are needed between now and
December 31.

 

 

 

Accomplishments

Since the development program was publicly
announced in 1972, progress has been steady, im-
pressive, and inspiring. There have been these
accomplishments:

e Construction of the new undergraduate li-
brary, a magnificent structure which was completed
in 1979 at a cost of $9.2 million;

e Construction of Lewis Hall, the ultramodern
$9-million law school building, which was formally
dedicated in May 1977;

® Construction of the J. W. Warner Athletic
Center, a five-floor athletic complex that includes a
2,500-seat gymnasium, a six-lane swimming pool,
and complete facilities for intercollegiate, intra-
mural and recreational activities;

@ Renovation and remodeling of the old library
building to become the home of the School of Com-
merce, Economics, and Politics;

@ Renovation and remodeling of both Tucker
Hall and Newcomb Hall to provide substantially
improved facilities for several undergraduate de-
partments and for the University’s computer center;

e A total of $18 million in commitments for
enlarging the University’s endowment, which,
among other progress, has made possible the crea-
tion of 66 new scholarships, six new professorships,
and three teaching funds;

e A tripling of the annual giving program, which
reached an all-time high in 1980-81 when gifts ex-
ceeded $1.1 million.

Decade-Long Development Program is Driving

Toward Successful Conclusion in December

Washington and Lee University—her trustees, alumni, parents and
friends—has been engaged in a comprehensive development program
which was announced publicly in 1972 and is to be finished by the end of
1981. The first phase of the program had a $36-million goal which was
achieved on time in December 1976. The second phase of the program, a
$26-million objective, is to be completed by December 1981, and gifts for
the $26-million phase had reached $21,115,919 as of June 30. At the time
of publication of this annual report $2 635,745 in additional
commitments have been received, making the total remaining objective
$2,248,336. As of June 30, these critically important capital objectives
remained:

Completion of the announced 1981 requirement
for endowment growth

Completion of the funding for the new home
of the School of Commerce, Economics,
and Politics

Final funding for the renovation of
Tucker and Newcomb Halls

As has always been the case, Annual Giving is the flagship of the fleet
in the overall success of the development program. The results of the
Annual Fund for 1981-82 will be added to the above capital objectives in
accounting for Washington and Lee’s total achievement.

Virginia
Foundation

for Independent
Colleges

An above-the-goal sum of
$2 .006,962 was contributed by
business and industry to 12 private
Virginia colleges through the VFIC
during 1980-81. Washington and
Lee University’s share was $187,077.
(Member colleges’ shares are based
on undergraduate enrollment.)

Under the direction of a board
that includes 35 business leaders and
the presidents of the 12 independent
colleges, the VFIC enlisted more
than 1,000 supporters, primarily
corporations doing business in
Virginia who are concerned with
strengthening private higher
education. Although, because of
space limitations, the full listing of
donor sources published annually in
ths VFIC annual report is not
repeated here, Washington and Lee
acknowledges with gratitude the
concern and leadership of the
companies, foundations, and
individuals, who made generous
contributions through the VFIC.

and Industry

was assisted in large measure during
1980-81 by direct grants from
corporations in addition to corporate
assistance received through the
Virginia Foundation for
Independent Colleges (VFIC).

private education take on special
meaning and importance. The total
of these gifts during the 1980-81
year was $34,050.
