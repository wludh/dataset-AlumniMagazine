O DEVELOPMENT of the recent years, it seems to me, holds a finer promise of
help for Washington and Lee than the class-agent plan for raising the alumni fund.
The method is simple; the burden upon any single alumnus, we hope, is never heavy;

the total results are of immense service to the University.

Beyond my power of expression is the sense of encouragement that stirred the
campus last year when we learned that more than 1000 alumni, widely scattered in
years, in professions, in residence, had contributed for the welfare of their Alma Mater.
And here must be recorded our gratitude not only to these donors but also to the of-

ficials who gave generous and effective service as the class representatives.

If this plan is helpful in normal times, it is vastly more significant to us in such a
period of emergency as we now have. The usual sources of revenue for the institution
have been reduced, seriously reduced. At the same time, our efforts to provide special
courses for our boys, and thus render maximum aid to our country, have cost money.
It may be assumed that the expense of rendering this kind of extraordinary opportun-

ity in connection with the National cause costs at least as much as an average depart-
ment.

We shall be strengthend by the response that the sons of Washington and Lee
may be able to offer to their school in the hour of acute need.

cur University had its origins in a time of national
O stress. The devoted men who raised the first funds
and those who contributed later to its endowment did so
‘n the faith that Washington and Lee would live as a
permanent institution. Its development and growth have
surpassed their fondest expectations.

To Washington and Lee men of today, again in a time
of great national stress, is given the opportunity to carry
on their work. In times like the present it is well that we
have things in which we can believe and depend upon to

endure—things we are resolved shall never be lost to us

 

er our children—the integrity of individuals and nations,
the sanctity of our homes and institutions, freedom of
worship, speech and thought. The history of Washington
and Lee is the best guarantee that it will carry on with
the task of instilling these principles into its students and
continue to build men for democracy. Washington and
Lee has emerged from every other national crisis, her
values intact and unimpaired. We Alumni must stand
behind her, now as in the past.

No student at Washington and Lee ever paid the
full cost of his tuition. Many of us paid all that was
charged on the bill, and many others were aided in part,
but no one was asked to pay the full cost of instruction,
of equipment, of maintenance, of depreciation, and of
interest on the investment. Notwithstanding increases in
the University endowment, the net income on this endow-
ment has decreased alarmingly. The Alumni Fund has
been and is essential in making both ends meet.

This is the tenth year of the Class Agent Plan and of
the Alumni Fund, made up of annual contributions from
loyal Washington and Lee men. Since its beginning in
1933, the Alumni Fund has grown in each succeeding

year and the report submitted herewith for 1941 shows

that 1039 Washington and Lee men, or 13 per cent parti-
cipated last year, contributing a total of $8,438.90. While
impressive, the fact remains that 87 per cent did not par-
ticipate.

Your Committee, meeting in Lexington on February
the 5th, carefully canvassed the needs and requirements
of the University for the ensuing year, in the light of the
present emergency. As Alumni know, the University itself
defrays all alumni expenses and the gifts of Alumni are
devoted to University purposes, approved and adminis-
tered by the Alumni Fund Committee. For 1942 three
outstanding needs have been listed. A card enclosed in
sach class letter gives you the opportunity of designating

to which one of these needs your gift will be allocated :

COMPLETION OF INTRAMURAL FIELD:
The projected new program of physical education, de-
signed for the war emergency, renders final completion

of the field, already in use, absolutely essential.

EMERGENCY EQUIPMENT AND SPECIAL
COURES FUND: Primarily to aid in the purchase of
special equipment needed for war courses, such as muili-
tary fundamentals, meteorology, military photography,
special maps and texts for courses in Naval history, Pa-
cific and Latin American geography, rifle range equip-

ment expense, and the like.

GENERAL UNRESTRICTED: Gitts so desig-
nated to be used for general purposes of University ex-

pense.

Our appeal is that you do your part by answering
promptly your Class Agent’s letter. Each and every con-
tribution is vital, and it is vital that every Washington

and Lee man make his contribution.
